What exactly is it, and how can you get involved?



On this page we'll explain all of the concepts behind BitCoin (and other concepts related to it) in the simplest possible terms to hopefully get you to a place where you feel comfortable in becoming part of the ever-expanding crypto community.

Think of your bank account.


With your bank, whether it be with Bank of America, Capital One, TD Bank, Wells Fargo, Citibank, or any other, your money is with them in their electronic system represented by numbers in their computers. You access your money using ATMs that are connected to their computers and know exactly how much money you have. This is called a centralized service. It is called a centralized service because the computers that know how much money each person has belongs solely to the bank and no other entity can access it besides you.


This sounds like a good system right?


Yes, it does, in a perfect world. You don't want other people to be able to access your money in your account right? However, we do not live in a perfect world, and due to the instability of existing infrastructure and distrust of large corporations, governments, and the banks themselves, people have turned to a safer, more secure option.

Why is this an issue?

Recently, confidence in large companies, governments, and banks have begun to faulter. Social networks are accused of removing content, banks and crediting agencies are becoming the victims of hacks on a scarily regular basis, and the government has been accused of spying on our internet habits, all with the help of these centralized service. If these companies and governments have access to our data (remember, our data is stored on their machines, the definition of a centralized service), they have free reigns to our lives online.

What's the Solution?

Imagine a scenario where instead of your computer talking to another single computer belonging to a company, bank, or government, it talked to many different computers belonging to people just like you and me. Instead of you posting a status update to Facebook, you would post to Nancy, Ben, Jeffrey, Tom, Jim, and 25 million other people, who would all now understand that you wished someone a happy birthday, and be able to confirm that you sent it if asked by someone else. Facebook or a government would not be able to take that post down if it was posted on half a million different computers that did not belong to them.

What is Decentralization?

The scenario described above is called decentralization. It is called that because there is no single computer that knows what you posted for sure, whether that be a financial transaction or a cat picture posted to social media, but 25 million computers can prove that you posted the same cat picture. Decentralization is based off of trust in numbers and multiple copies of the same information.

But wait, that sounds dangerous!


For clarity: Never put your current financial information on the web or send it to a friend. This is a critical difference between centralized and decentralized services. All major banks are centralized and therefore transparency does not apply.


With decentralized services, the more people that know the correct information the more secure that information is and the less likely it is to change without your permission. If one person wants to hack your bank account they simply need a username and password, but when it comes to decentralized services, it is nearly impossible to be "hacked" without the hacker being able to retrieve a long and complicated text file called a private key that belongs to you. If a hacker can access this key file and your data, they can make requests to the blockchain on your behalf, such as transferring coins.

Like the one to my door?

So what IS Bitcoin?

Now that you understand the idea of decentralization, we can start to talk about the specific implementations of it, including Bitcoin. Bitcoin is based on "blockchain" technology. Every coin, Bitcoin, Ethereum, LiteCoin, DogeCoin, Ripple, MobileCoin and so forth have their own ledger called a blockchain that keeps track of transactions on the network, and how many coins everyone has. Remember above when we talked about trust and how if enough computers confirm something to be true, it is? The same thinking applies here. When people send or receive Bitcoin (or others, they will be collectively referred to as cryptocurrency from here on out), it is logged in that cryptocurrency's blockchain and confirmed by a bunch of other computers to be legitimate (if the transaction is proven to be yours with your private key). Cryptocurrencies are stored in wallets, which are programs designed to keep track of your cryptocurrency on computers.

How do I get cryptocurrency?

There are two ways to get cryptocurrency outside of receiving it from someone else. In the early days, you would run a computer program that may or may not yield cryptocurrency. This was called "mining", and it would use up all of your computer's energy and sometimes get you some cryptocurrency. As coins have become more popular, mining has become less effective, and it is much more common for people to use exchanges, which are websites where you can exchange fiat (USD, EUR, etc) for cryptocurrency. Read on for more information about exchanges.

Exchanges?

Excahnges are websites that allow you to deposit fiat currency (USD/EUR/etc) and retreive cryptocurrency in return. Trustworthy websites (that you should probably stick to) are Coinbase, BitStamp, and Kraken. While simple in concept, it is important to know that these websites are centralized services (remember these? They are services that host your data on their computers instead of your own), and therefore should not be completely trusted with your coins for long periods of time. Exchanges use wallets just like your computer, but store everyone's cryptocurrency and only allow you to access yours through their website. You should transfer cryptocurrency to your own wallet if you plan on holding them for an extended period of time. Centralized services are much easier to hack, remember?

Why Cryptocurrency?

Cryptocurrency is the next generation of finance. As with anything, it has a list of pros and cons attached to it.


Pros


Trustworthy

As we've seen, cryptocurrency is based on the concept of decentralization which is more trustworthy than centralized services as your informaton cannot be changed by anyone but someone with your secure private key.



Popular

Cryptocurrency is currently one of the hottest topics in the news and online right now, if it wasn't you probably wouldn't be reading this page right now. This means there are plenty of resources (including this one) for the community to grow.



Free

Cryptocurrency encourages liberty due to its lack of regulation in most places and its core concept of decentralization. This allows more freedom and flexibility when it comes to spending, investing, and saving.



Legal

While a gray area in taxation, and while there are plenty of illegal uses for cryptocurrency, there are regulations and fraud prevention measures in most exchanges. Be sure to check with the laws in your country and state (if applicable) before investing.

Cons


Volatile

Due to its surge in popularity, cryptocurrency's price is currently highly volatile, meaning it can drop and rise hundreds of dollars in minutes. Be sure to consult with a financial advisor before investing. Invest responsibly.



Unbacked

While the same argument could be made for the US dollar, the price of cryptocurrency is not backed by any government or bank, and therefore is uninsured and unregulated. Again, invest responsibly.



Targeted

Due to the first two cons, hackers are often targeting holders of cryptocurrency as it is uninsured and the easiest way to make a profit. The concept of decentralization being trustworthy and unhackable only holds true for you if your private keys stay secure and your coins actually belong to your private keys.



Stigma

When most people think of cryptocurrency, most people think of the black market. While cryptocurrency is popular due to it being unregulated and mostly unmonitored, there are perfectly legal ways to use cryptocurrency, including online shopping, paying friends, and exchanging for cash in real life.
